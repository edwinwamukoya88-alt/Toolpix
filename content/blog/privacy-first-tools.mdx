## The State of Online Privacy

Every action you take online generates data. When you use a typical web tool — a document converter, image editor, or note-taking app — your data often travels through external servers, gets stored, analyzed, and sometimes sold.

In 2026, data privacy is more critical than ever:

- Data breaches expose billions of records annually
- Third-party tracking is embedded in most websites
- Cloud services can access your uploaded files
- Your personal data is used to train AI models without consent

## The Problem with Traditional Web Tools

Most online tools operate on a server-based model:

1. **You upload** your file or enter your data
2. **The server processes** it remotely
3. **The result** is sent back to you
4. **Your data** remains on the server (often indefinitely)

This creates several privacy risks:

### Data Retention

Even if a service promises to delete your files, there's no guarantee. Copies may exist in backups, caches, or logs.

### Third-Party Access

Many tools use third-party processing services, meaning your data passes through multiple systems you don't control.

### Legal Jurisdiction

Data stored in another country may be subject to different privacy laws, including government access without your knowledge.

### Targeted Advertising

Some free tools monetize by analyzing your data and serving targeted ads or selling insights to marketers.

## The Privacy-First Alternative

Privacy-first tools process everything **locally in your browser**. Here's how they work:

### Client-Side Processing

All computation happens in your browser using JavaScript. Your data never leaves your device.

### No Server Upload

Files, text, and images stay on your computer. There's nothing to delete because nothing was stored.

### Works Offline

Many privacy-first tools work without an internet connection after the initial page load.

## What to Look for in Privacy-First Tools

When evaluating any online tool, check for:

1. **Client-side processing** — Verify the tool works without sending data to a server
2. **No account requirement** — If you need to sign up, your data is being stored
3. **Clear privacy policy** — Look for explicit statements about data handling
4. **Open source** — Publicly auditable code is more trustworthy
5. **Offline capability** — A good sign that processing happens locally
